The Problem This Solves

Most organizations have pricing. Few have a pricing system. The difference appears as margin erosion that nobody can explain, Sales-Finance conflict with no resolution mechanism, and deals that win on revenue but lose on profit. A pricing function without architecture is just a series of one-off decisions — each defensible in isolation, collectively destructive.

How It Gets Done
Price-Volume-Mix (PVM) Analysis Decomposing revenue changes to determine whether growth is driven by price, volume, or mix — critical for separating real pricing performance from favorable conditions.
Annual Pricing Cycle Ownership End-to-end management from competitive benchmarking through Finance alignment, Sales rollout, and post-launch realization tracking.
Pricing Council Design & Facilitation Building the governance structure — membership, cadence, decision rights — that converts pricing from a reactive bottleneck into a proactive strategic function.
SKU Rationalization & Rate Card Architecture 1,000+ SKUs is not a catalog — it's a liability. Collapsing complexity into scalable Good-Better-Best structures that Sales can actually sell and Finance can actually manage.
Usage-Based Monetization Design Aligning the value metric — per-seat, per-API-call, per-row-processed — to the customer's economic reality and the company's cost structure simultaneously.
Competitive Benchmarking & Price Positioning Primary and secondary research to map competitor price points, packaging traps, and gaps — not to match the market, but to understand where differentiated pricing is defensible.
Price Elasticity & Willingness-to-Pay Research Quantitative and qualitative analysis of price sensitivity by segment to inform both list price architecture and discount band design.
Price Increase Strategy & Execution Designing the increase, securing the approvals, building the Sales communication, and tracking realization — including managing the inevitable pushback from Sales leadership.

Pricing analytics that only describes what happened is a history lesson. The organizations that use analytics as a competitive weapon are the ones asking different questions: Where is margin leaking and why? Which reps are over-discounting and what are they being told by procurement? What does a 2% price increase actually do to churn in segment three? How It Gets Done
Price Realization Analysis Measuring the gap between list price and pocket price — after every discount, rebate, and concession. The waterfall that reveals where value actually disappears.
Discount Leakage Audits Identifying specific regions, reps, and products where discounting exceeds peer benchmarks — with enough specificity to drive a targeted conversation, not a company-wide edict.
Win/Loss Analysis Synthesizing CRM data and field feedback to understand why deals are lost — to price, to a competitor, or to no-decision. Three very different problems with very different solutions.
Dynamic Pricing Models Scenario modeling that answers real executive questions: "What happens to revenue if list price increases 5% but churn increases 1%?" Built in Excel for accessibility, not locked in a BI tool no one opens.
Executive Dashboard Design (Power BI / Tableau) Dashboards built around decisions, not data. The question before every visualization: what action does a VP take differently if this number moves?
Multi-System Data Reconciliation When Salesforce Opportunity ID ≠ CPQ Quote ID ≠ Finance Order ID, the analysis only works if someone builds the master linking table and validates it. That's the unglamorous work that makes everything else credible.
Segment & Product Profitability Analysis Understanding which customers, geographies, and products are actually profitable — and which are consuming Deal Desk capacity for deals that shouldn't be prioritized.
Price-Volume-Mix (PVM) Decomposition Separating the contribution of pricing decisions from volume changes and product mix shifts — the essential diagnostic before any pricing recommendation.

The Problem This Solves

Value-based pricing fails in practice when it stays theoretical. Organizations adopt the language — "we price to value" — without the mechanics that make it defensible in a live negotiation. The real work is translating customer value into a number that Sales can walk into a deal with, Finance can model, and procurement can't easily dismantle. That requires frameworks, not just philosophy.

How It Gets Done
Economic Value Estimation (EVE) Quantifying the economic value of a product relative to the next-best alternative — the foundation of a price that can be defended on the customer's own financial terms.
Value Driver Identification & Mapping Identifying which product capabilities actually move the customer's economics — revenue, cost reduction, risk mitigation — and which are features the pricing team is overvaluing.
Willingness-to-Pay Research Primary and secondary research across customer segments to establish where price sensitivity inflects — informing both list price architecture and the design of discount bands.
TCO Modeling (Customer Perspective) Total cost of ownership analysis built from the customer's viewpoint — including implementation, training, integration, and switching costs — that reframes the price conversation entirely.
Competitive Value Gap Quantification Translating feature differences into dollar terms that Sales can deploy when a competitor claims price parity on a functionally inferior product.
Customer ROI Calculator Development Interactive tools that quantify the cost of inaction — not to replace the sales conversation, but to give the customer's internal champion a number to defend internally.
Segment-Specific Value Modeling The same product creates different value in manufacturing vs. professional services vs. healthcare. Building the segment models that price accordingly rather than averaging across them.
Value Realization Tracking Measuring whether customers are actually capturing the value that justified the price — a feedback loop into renewal pricing, expansion pricing, and future product monetization.
Proven At

AT&T's Office@Hand Sales faced a consistent procurement tactic: position Microsoft Teams as "free" to force discounting. The counter-strategy wasn't a price concession — it was a TCO framework that quantified total implementation cost, integration complexity, and support economics. Combined with competitive battlecards built around the specific objection, the value-based approach drove margin from 12% to 18% without product changes or price restructuring. The price held. The narrative changed.

The Problem This Solves

SaaS pricing is uniquely unforgiving because the wrong value metric compounds across thousands of contracts simultaneously. A misaligned pricing model doesn't create a bad quarter — it creates a structural profitability problem that takes years to unwind. The organizations that get this right aren't just choosing between per-seat and usage-based. They're asking which metric best captures the value the customer receives and aligns with the cost the company incurs. Those are often different questions with different answers.

How It Gets Done
Value Metric Selection & Alignment Evaluating per-seat, per-unit, per-API-call, per-row-processed, and hybrid models against both customer value capture and company cost structure — choosing the metric that scales with the right variable.
Price Migration Strategy Moving customers from a legacy model to a new one without triggering churn, contract disputes, or Sales rebellion — grandfather clauses, migration incentives, and phased transition design.
Net Revenue Retention (NRR) Optimization Designing expansion pricing — upsell triggers, usage thresholds, cross-sell structures — so that customer growth translates into revenue growth without requiring a new sales motion.
Partner & Reseller Economics Three-party pricing where the reseller's margin floor, the end customer's price ceiling, and the vendor's cost floor all have to coexist. Modeling the economics before the channel agreement is signed.
Packaging & Feature Gating Determining which capabilities belong in which tier — and which features, if gated correctly, create upgrade pressure rather than customer frustration.
SaaS Metrics Fluency ARR, MRR, ACV, TCV, NRR, GRR, CAC payback, LTV/CAC — understanding which metrics matter to which stakeholder and how pricing decisions flow through each of them.
Contract Structure Design ELA, ELP, pay-as-you-go, hybrid commit-plus-overage — designing the contract structure that captures committed revenue while preserving upside from high-usage customers.
Revenue Recognition Considerations (ASC 606) Understanding how pricing model decisions — bundling, performance obligations, variable consideration — affect revenue recognition timing and financial reporting.
Proven At

At Sabre, the per-passenger pricing model was misaligned with cost structure — API calls were the actual cost driver, not passengers boarded. Designed a two-track migration: a preferred per-API-call model for new contracts and a legacy bridge with overage protection for existing customers. 60% migrated within 18 months. A second-order effect emerged: airlines needed help optimizing their API usage patterns, which created an unplanned professional services revenue stream. The monetization model changed how customers used the product — and how they valued it.

The Problem This Solves

Pricing leaders rarely have direct authority over the functions they depend on most. Sales sets the expectation. Finance owns the margin target. Product controls the roadmap. Legal approves the terms. Operations determines what's actually deliverable. The pricing function sits at the intersection of all of them and controls none of them. The leaders who build effective pricing organizations understand that the job is credibility, not authority — and that credibility is earned through data, delivery, and showing up prepared.

How It Gets Done
Pricing Center of Excellence (COE) Design Building the org structure, governance model, decision rights, and operating cadence for a pricing function that outlasts its founder — not a team of one, but a durable organizational capability.
Pricing Council Facilitation Running the executive forum — CFO, CRO, CPO — where pricing strategy shifts are reviewed and approved. Preparing the agenda, managing the pre-read, and ensuring decisions get made rather than deferred.
RACI Matrix & Governance Framework Design Clarifying who decides, who inputs, who executes, and who needs to know — for every pricing decision type, at every deal size threshold. Governance that prevents the "who approved this?" conversation.
Executive Communication & Data Storytelling Translating pricing analytics into executive narratives — the difference between "here is the data" and "here is what we should do about it and why it matters now."
Change Management Strategy When pricing governance changes, Sales pushes back. When CPQ launches, reps find workarounds. Managing the adoption curve — anticipating the resistance, designing the response — is as important as the change itself.
Train-the-Trainer Program Design Building institutional knowledge that survives personnel transitions — documentation, certification, and backup trainer development designed to outlast the person who built it.
SOPs & Pricing Playbook Development The operational documentation that converts tribal knowledge into repeatable process — built for the person who joins six months from now, not just the team that exists today.
Nonprofit Board Governance Directing a $55K annual scholarship program for a 5,000-member 501(c)(3) — fiduciary accountability, board reporting, and volunteer team leadership without compensation leverage. The influence-without-authority challenge at its most pure.
Proven At

During AT&T's three-phase downsizing — 80 Deal Desk professionals reduced to 50, with 95% turnover — rebuilt the onboarding program from scattered PowerPoint decks into a 3-week curriculum: 15 training decks, 25+ recorded sessions, formal certification at 85% passing threshold. Designed explicitly to outlast the builder through train-the-trainer methodology. At the Fortune 50 Tech Company engagement, designed the Pricing COE org structure, RACI matrix, and governance model as a deliverable — not just a recommendation. Sabre: API Re-Monetization

Company: Sabre Corporation (B2B SaaS Airline Solutions) | Timeline: 2019-2021

The Pricing Misalignment

Sabre's Passenger Service System (PSS) was priced per-passenger-boarded, but profitability was actually driven by API calls (millions monthly). Airlines with high API usage relative to passengers were unprofitable customers, while the pricing model couldn't capture the value.

Strategic Insight: Product team analysis showed inconsistent profitability. Finance revealed costs tied to API calls (reservation creation, cancellations, modifications) not passengers. Value metric misaligned with cost structure.

My Approach

  • Data Analysis: Categorized millions of monthly API calls by value (creation vs. cancellation vs. check-in), worked with Finance for revenue recognition compliance
  • Two-Track Pricing: (A) Preferred: per-API-call with tiered rates, (B) Legacy bridge: per-passenger with overage fees for excessive API usage
  • Migration Strategy: Grandfather existing customers on Track B, incent new customers to Track A, provide migration path

Impact

  • 60% of customers migrated to Track A (per-API-call) within 18 months
  • 40% stayed on Track B but overage fees protected margin on high-API-usage customers
  • Accidentally created professional services revenue stream (airlines needed help optimizing API usage)

Additional Achievements at Sabre

10% Margin Uplift Discovery: Identified underpriced product through analytics - product with <5% Custom Deal Desk involvement meant Sales wasn't negotiating (product too easy to sell). Recommended 10% price increase despite Sales resistance ("You're taking away our one easy sell!"). Secured VP approval, result: immediate 10% margin uplift on 95% of deals with minimal win rate impact (85% → 82%).

Pricing Council Leadership: Chaired monthly Pricing Council for PSS products, collaborating with Product, Finance, Sales, Engineering on strategic pricing decisions.
